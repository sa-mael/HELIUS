Metaphysics: Study of Being as Being

Metaphysics inquires into first principles and causes. It does not ask about one craft or species, but about what belongs to all things insofar as they are.

Proem

Humans seek knowledge by nature. We begin with sense, then memory, then experience. Wisdom asks “because of what?” in the most general way. This inquiry is first philosophy.

I. Substance and the Structure of Being (Ontology)

What most truly exists is substance: the “this” that underlies and persists. Natural substances are composites of matter and form.

  • Matter: that out of which a thing is made.
  • Form: what makes it the kind of thing it is.
  • Composite: the unified whole here and now.

Universals are said of substances, but primary reality lies in individual substances on which other things depend.

II. The Four Causes and Explanation

  1. Material cause: what it is made from.
  2. Formal cause: what it is to be that thing (its essence).
  3. Efficient cause: the source of change.
  4. Final cause: the end for the sake of which it is.

Where there is order, ends are principles, not ornaments. Explanation is complete only when the end is clear.

III. Possibility, Necessity, and Law (Modality)

A thing is possible when it has the capacity to be; necessary when it cannot be otherwise given its nature.

  • Natural necessity: from what a thing is.
  • Hypothetical necessity: from an end chosen; if the end is set, the means are necessary.

What we call laws summarize these necessities, whose ground is in natures and forms, not in words.

IV. Identity, Change, and Persistence

Change is the actualization of what exists potentially, insofar as it is potential.

  • Generation: coming-to-be when form informs matter.
  • Alteration: change in quality while substance remains.
  • Locomotion: change in place.

A thing remains the same when its ruling form is one and continuous. Identity follows organized form.

V. Causation, Powers, and Explanation

Causes are per se when they belong to a thing as such, and accidental when by coincidence. Powers are directed tendencies rooted in form.

Explanation seeks the proper “because” at each level: elements by natures, organisms by ends, artifacts by designs.

VI. Space and Time

  • Place: the inner boundary of the containing body.
  • Time: the number of motion with respect to before and after; it depends on change and a soul that counts.

VII. Mind and Matter (Soul and World)

Living things are hylomorphic: body as matter, soul as form. Nutrition, perception, and thought are powers of one living substance.

Intellect knows by becoming its object in act: potentially all forms, actually this form or that in understanding.

VIII. Method: From Appearances to Principles

Begin from reputable opinions and appearances. Preserve what is true in each and resolve conflicts by better causes. Demonstrate where possible; for first principles, proceed to what is simpler and prior.

IX. The Highest Cause

Series of movers do not explain themselves. There is an unmoved mover: pure actuality, cause as final rather than pushing. As the intelligible object of desire and thought, it explains motion and order.

X. Summary Map for Study

  • Ontology: substance; matter–form; categories.
  • Causes: material, formal, efficient, final.
  • Modality: potentiality–actuality; necessity–possibility.
  • Identity and Change: persistence by form; kinds of change.
  • Causation and Powers: per se vs accidental; teleology.
  • Space and Time: place as boundary; time as numbered motion.
  • Mind and Matter: soul as form; intellect as potentially all forms.
  • First Principle: unmoved mover as final cause.

Closing

Metaphysics studies being as being and the attributes that belong to it. It asks what things are, why they are, and for the sake of what. Its end is knowledge of the highest causes, which orders the rest.

Core questions

  • What kinds of things are fundamental?
  • Are numbers, properties, or universals real?
  • What is a law of nature?
  • Could the world have been otherwise?
  • What is personal identity across time?
  • Do causes produce effects or merely correlate?
  • Is time tensed or tenseless?
  • How does mind relate to brain?

Subfields

Ontology

Categories of being, mereology, realism vs. nominalism.

Modality

Possible worlds, essence, counterfactuals.

Identity & change

Persistence, endurantism vs. perdurantism.

Causation

Regularity, counterfactual, powers; causal graphs.

Space & time

Substantivalism vs. relationism; presentism vs. eternalism.

Mind & world

Dualism, physicalism, panpsychism.

Methods

  • Conceptual analysis: clarify terms and commitments.
  • Thought experiments: test intuitions under variation.
  • Formal tools: modal logic, sets, dependence models.
  • Inference to best explanation: weigh cost vs. power.
  • Cross-checks: coherence with science and language.

Relations to science

Metaphysics interprets and organizes scientific results rather than duplicating them.

Critiques & limits

  • Verificationism: non-testable claims risk meaninglessness.
  • Quine: ontology should follow best science.
  • Method: unchecked intuitions mislead.

Study roadmap

  2. Classic: Aristotle or Descartes (selected).
  3. Modern: van Inwagen; Lowe.
  4. Method: Williamson on methodology.
  5. Project: one thesis card with sources.

FAQs

Is metaphysics obsolete after modern physics?

No. Physics is empirical; metaphysics clarifies categories.

Isn’t it just speculation?

Good work states assumptions, argues, and respects science.
